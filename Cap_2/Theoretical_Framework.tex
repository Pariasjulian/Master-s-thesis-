\chapter{Theoretical Framework}
\label{ch:theoretical_framework}

As established in the problem statement, the clinical translation of continuous neurocognitive assessments faces two sequential, critical failures: the corruption of the analog signal due to SNR limitations in embedded systems, and the temporal misalignment of biomarkers caused by non-deterministic synchronization. Addressing these failures requires a multidisciplinary foundation spanning neurophysiology, precision electronic engineering, and real-time computing. This chapter systematizes the theoretical concepts underpinning the development of the proposed EEG acquisition framework. It begins by defining the neurophysiological signals of interest---Event-Related Potentials and spontaneous alpha rhythms---and their clinical relevance to ADHD, thus establishing \textit{what} must be measured. It then formalizes the physical and electrical principles governing signal capture at the electrode-skin interface and within mixed-signal hardware, establishing \textit{how} these signals are acquired and why SNR preservation is critical. Finally, it addresses the principles of data synchronization and real-time determinism, establishing \textit{when} events must be temporally locked to physiological data to ensure clinical validity.

\section{Neurophysiology and Event-Related Potentials (ERPs)}
\label{sec:neurophysiology}

Electroencephalography (EEG) constitutes a non-invasive technique for recording cerebral bioelectric activity via transducers arranged on the scalp. While continuous EEG analysis allows for the monitoring of basal brain states—such as wakefulness, sleep, or convulsive pathologies—cognitive neuroscience research requires isolating specific neuronal responses associated with sensory, motor, or cognitive stimuli. These voltage fluctuations, known as Event-Related Potentials (ERPs), represent the synchronized activity of pyramidal neuronal populations in response to information processing.

    \centering

    \caption{Characteristic morphology of an Event-Related Potential (ERP), highlighting exogenous and endogenous components such as the N200 and P300.}
    \label{fig:p300_waveform}
\end{figure}

Within the complex morphology of ERPs, two endogenous components are of particular interest for neurocognitive evaluation and the implementation of serious games in the context of this project. The first is the N200 (or N2) component, a negative deflection that reaches its maximum amplitude between 200 and 350 ms post-stimulus. This component is functionally linked to executive control, specifically in mismatch detection processes and the inhibition of motor responses. The second component, the P300 (or P3b), manifests as a prominent positive deflection with a latency of 300 to 600 ms. Its amplitude is modulated by the allocation of attentional resources and the updating of working memory, being particularly sensitive to stimulus improbability (the \textit{oddball} paradigm). Due to these characteristics, the P300 is consolidated as a robust biomarker for quantifying cognitive load and attentional deficits.

The detection of these components presents a significant challenge in signal processing due to their low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). ERPs possess typical amplitudes in the range of $1\mu V$ to $20\mu V$, frequently remaining masked by background EEG activity, the magnitude of which oscillates between $50\mu V$ and $100\mu V$. To extract the signal of interest, the technique of coherent signal averaging is employed. Assuming that background noise is a stochastic process with zero mean and is uncorrelated with the stimulus, by averaging $N$ trials, the noise amplitude decreases in proportion to $1/\sqrt{N}$, while the ERP signal remains constant.

However, the validity of this technique depends strictly on temporal stability. Variability in the synchronization marker's latency, a phenomenon termed \textit{jitter}, introduces systematic errors in the resulting average. Mathematically, if the trigger latency follows a normal distribution with standard deviation $\sigma_t$, the averaging process acts as a low-pass filter on the original waveform, attenuating high-frequency components and distorting peak amplitude. A jitter greater than 10 ms ($\sigma_t > 10$ ms) is sufficient to degrade the morphology of the N200 component, compromising the diagnostic utility of the data. Consequently, acquisition systems must guarantee strict real-time (\textit{hard real-time}) synchronization to preserve the spectral and temporal integrity of the biomarkers. This requirement motivates the hardware-level temporal synchronization framework proposed in this thesis, which implements a deterministic event injection mechanism at the microcontroller level to eliminate software-induced jitter.

Beyond these transient, stimulus-locked potentials, the validation of any acquisition system also requires proving its ability to capture ongoing, spontaneous cortical rhythms. The following section introduces the alpha rhythm and its characteristic blocking response, which serves as the foundational physiological proof-of-concept for the proposed architecture.

\section{Spontaneous EEG Activity and Alpha Blocking}
\label{sec:alpha_blocking}

In addition to transient event-related potentials, the continuous EEG signal is characterized by rhythmic, spontaneous oscillations reflecting ongoing cortical states. Among these, the alpha rhythm (8--13 Hz) is the most prominent feature of the waking background EEG. Primarily originating from the occipital and posterior parietal lobes, alpha activity is highly pronounced during relaxed wakefulness with closed eyes.

A defining characteristic of this rhythm is its reactivity to visual stimulation and cognitive engagement. When a subject opens their eyes or engages in visual or mental tasks, the amplitude of the alpha waves visibly and rapidly attenuates---a phenomenon known as \textit{alpha blocking} or the Berger effect. This suppression represents a shift from a synchronized idling state of the cortical networks to a desynchronized, active processing state. In the context of EEG system validation, the reliable detection of occipital alpha blocking serves as a fundamental physiological proof-of-concept, confirming the system's end-to-end capability to capture true neurophysiological responses rather than environmental artifacts. As described in the problem statement, this baseline test is a necessary precondition before extracting complex ERPs, and its execution within the proposed architecture is validated through spectral analysis of eyes-open versus eyes-closed conditions to confirm end-to-end acquisition fidelity.

Having established the neurophysiological signals that the acquisition system must faithfully capture, the following section contextualizes these signals within the specific clinical domain targeted by this thesis: the electrophysiological assessment of ADHD.

\section{Electrophysiological Manifestations of ADHD}
\label{sec:adhd_manifestations}

Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) is a neurodevelopmental condition characterized by persistent patterns of inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity. Clinically, the diagnosis of ADHD relies heavily on behavioral observations and subjective rating scales. However, electrophysiological techniques offer objective biomarkers that reflect the underlying neurological atypicalities associated with the disorder.

In resting-state EEG, individuals with ADHD frequently exhibit an elevated Theta/Beta ratio (TBR) compared to neurotypical peers. This ratio reflects an excess of slow-wave (Theta) activity, often associated with cortical underarousal or drowsiness, and a deficit in fast-wave (Beta) activity, which is linked to active concentration and cognitive engagement. While the TBR has been extensively studied as a potential diagnostic metric, its clinical utility is complemented by the analysis of dynamic brain responses during cognitive tasks.

During cognitive paradigms, such as continuous performance tests (CPTs) or the \textit{oddball} task, ERPs provide a window into the temporal dynamics of information processing in ADHD. The most consistent finding is a reduction in the amplitude of the P300 component. This attenuation is theorized to reflect deficits in the allocation of attentional resources and the updating of context in working memory. Furthermore, abnormalities in the N200 component are frequently observed, particularly during tasks requiring inhibitory control (e.g., Go/No-Go tasks). A reduced N200 amplitude in these contexts suggests difficulties in conflict monitoring and the suppression of prepotent motor responses, aligning with the impulsivity characteristic of the disorder.

Continuous EEG monitoring coupled with interactive tasks (such as the serious games within the ACEMATE framework) aims to systematically elicit and quantify these electrophysiological markers, providing a quantitative basis for assessing cognitive performance and the efficacy of therapeutic interventions in ADHD. The sensitivity of these biomarkers to both signal noise and temporal jitter underscores the strict engineering requirements that the proposed MONEEE system must satisfy: the hardware must preserve the microvolt-level fidelity of P300 and N200 components, and the synchronization framework must guarantee sub-millisecond temporal alignment to prevent morphological distortion during signal averaging.

With the clinical targets clearly defined, the discussion now turns to the physical and electrical foundations of signal acquisition, beginning with the critical electrode-skin interface that constitutes the first point of contact between the biological and electronic domains.

\section{The Electrode-Skin Interface and Signal Integrity}
\label{sec:electrode_skin}

The acquisition of bioelectric potentials begins at the physical interface between the measuring electrode and the scalp. This interface effectively transduces ionic currents generated by neuronal activity into electron currents processed by the electronic circuitry. Electrically, this junction can be modeled as a parallel combination of a resistor and a capacitor (representing the charge double layer), in series with the skin's inherent epidermal resistance.

Achieving a low and balanced electrode-skin impedance is critical for signal fidelity. High contact impedance introduces several detrimental effects: it severely attenuates the microvolt-level EEG signal, significantly increases Johnson-Nyquist thermal noise, and exacerbates susceptibility to external electromagnetic interference via capacitive coupling. Consequently, clinical standards typically mandate maintaining contact impedances below 10 $k\Omega$. Modern acquisition systems incorporate active impedance monitoring, which operates by injecting a known, microscopic alternating current (e.g., at a frequency outside the bands of interest) through the electrodes. By continuously measuring the resulting voltage drop across the interface, the system can dynamically calculate the impedance, allowing operators to verify and adjust the physical setup before initiating sensitive cognitive assessments. These principles directly inform the optimization of the physical interface and analog capture, which is addressed through the design of a rapidly deployable EEG cap and a systematic impedance measurement protocol that minimizes setup friction and maximizes the initial signal-to-noise ratio.

Once the physical interface is established with high fidelity, the acquired analog signals must be digitized and managed by the embedded hardware. The following section presents the principles governing the design of mixed-signal acquisition systems, where preserving the integrity of microvolt-level signals within a digitally noisy environment constitutes a fundamental engineering challenge.

\section{Principles of Mixed-Signal Embedded Hardware Design}
\label{sec:hardware_principles}

The fidelity in the digitization of biopotentials is determined by the topology of the Analog Front-End (AFE). Modern acquisition architectures integrate specific analog-to-digital converters (ADCs) designed for biomedical instrumentation, which typically implement a Delta-Sigma ($\Delta\Sigma$) modulation architecture rather than traditional Successive Approximation Register (SAR) converters.

The $\Delta\Sigma$ architecture offers superior advantages in terms of dynamic range and noise rejection through two main mechanisms: oversampling and noise shaping. The device samples the input signal at a modulation frequency ($f_{mod}$) significantly higher than the Nyquist rate, distributing quantization noise power over a wider spectrum. Subsequently, the modulator shifts this noise toward high frequencies, outside the biological band of interest (0--100 Hz), allowing a digital decimation filter to eliminate it effectively while reducing the data rate to the output frequency configured by the user.

    \centering

    \caption{Simplified functional scheme of the modulation and filtering stage in a Delta-Sigma architecture ADC.}
    \label{fig:delta_sigma}
\end{figure}

A critical aspect for functional connectivity and EEG coherence analysis is sampling simultaneity. In older multiplexed systems, a single ADC core switches sequentially between channels, introducing a systematic phase delay ($t_{skew}$) between electrodes. Modern biomedical ADCs mitigate this problem by incorporating independent $\Delta\Sigma$ modulators for each channel, guaranteeing a virtually null $t_{skew}$ and preserving the real phase relationship between different cortical regions.

Even with optimized analog-to-digital converters, the acquisition of biopotentials is highly sensitive to external electromagnetic interference, most notably the 50/60 Hz mains power line noise. Because this interference typically couples equally to all recording electrodes and the patient's body, it manifests as a common-mode voltage. To actively suppress this interference, modern Analog Front-Ends employ a Driven Right Leg (DRL) circuit topology. Traditional grounding methods passively connect the patient to the system reference, which is often insufficient against high-amplitude transient noise. In contrast, a DRL circuit continuously acts as a negative feedback loop: it measures the common-mode voltage present at the recording electrodes, inverts and amplifies this signal, and actively drives it back into the patient's body through a dedicated reference electrode. This active cancellation significantly increases the system's overall Common-Mode Rejection Ratio (CMRR), ensuring that the minute differential EEG signals are not saturated or distorted by environmental noise.

To manage data flow without sacrificing temporal determinism, advanced acquisition designs adopt a heterogeneous computing architecture that decouples acquisition from high-level processing. This structure typically comprises a Microcontroller Unit (MCU) operating in real-time, coupled with a Microprocessor Unit (MPU) for complex application tasks. The MCU operates under strict real-time constraints (either on \textit{bare-metal} or with a lightweight RTOS), reacting to ADC hardware interrupts on microsecond scales to capture and timestamp samples without buffer overflows. Meanwhile, the MPU manages computationally intensive and non-deterministic tasks, such as protocol stacks and file system storage. This division of responsibilities isolates bio-signal acquisition from the variable latencies introduced by high-level operating system schedulers, ensuring data temporal integrity. These hardware design principles constitute the theoretical basis for the robust embedded acquisition architecture proposed in this thesis, which is concretely realized through the MONEEE system's heterogeneous TM4C1294/Raspberry Pi CM4 topology with galvanic isolation, active noise mitigation, and continuous resource monitoring.

While the preceding sections have addressed the acquisition of a high-fidelity signal, clinical neurocognitive assessment further demands that the recorded physiological data be precisely aligned with external cognitive stimuli. The following sections formalize the principles of data synchronization and real-time determinism that underpin the temporal synchronization strategy of this thesis.

\section{Principles of Data Synchronization}
\label{sec:sync_principles}

Precise synchronization between physiological recordings and external events (such as visual/auditory stimuli in a game) constitutes a central technical challenge in BCI and neurocognitive assessment systems. The selection of a synchronization method implies a trade-off between temporal precision, implementation complexity, and intrusion into the user experience. There are contrasting approaches to addressing this problem. Optical synchronization, based on photodiodes attached to the monitor, is considered the ``gold standard'' for validation, as it detects the physical change of pixels, bypassing software, operating system, and GPU rendering latencies. However, its requirement for external hardware limits its viability in massive clinical deployments. As a scalable alternative, the \textit{Lab Streaming Layer} (LSL) protocol offers a middleware solution that unifies disparate data streams by assigning timestamps referenced to a common clock and drift correction algorithms. While LSL simplifies integration, its final accuracy remains dependent on local network stability and the stimulation engine's ability to report the event time accurately.

In customized physical interfaces, standard buses like USB introduce non-trivial latency considerations, especially for the transmission of marking commands (\textit{soft-triggers}) from a PC or tablet to an amplifier. As a host-controlled bus utilizing polling, data transfer is discretized into frame intervals (1 ms in \textit{Full Speed}) or microframes ($125 \mu s$ in \textit{High Speed}). Additionally, data traverses the operating system driver stack, where it may be stored in intermediate buffers to optimize global system performance. This behavior introduces variable and unpredictable latencies of several milliseconds between the logical generation of an event and its physical arrival at the bus, which is fundamentally incompatible with the precision requirements for high-frequency ERP component analysis. To mitigate this latency indeterminacy, modern embedded designs must implement hardware-level synchronization protocols that couple event markers directly to biological samples at the lowest possible layer before routing to non-deterministic operating systems. The hardware event injection strategy adopted by the MONEEE system directly addresses this requirement by concatenating event markers into the EEG data frame at the microcontroller level, binding them to physiological samples before any data enters the non-deterministic Linux environment.

\section{Real-Time Performance and Determinism in Embedded Systems}
\label{sec:real_time_perf}

The continuous recording of multichannel, high-resolution EEG data generates a demanding, time-critical data stream. In embedded architectures acting as data loggers, managing this stream requires strict computational determinism to prevent buffer overflows and data loss. General-purpose operating systems, while adept at handling concurrent tasks like networking and user interfaces, employ ``fair-share'' task schedulers that can introduce unbounded, non-deterministic latencies during interrupt handling or thread context switching.

When the computational load (CPU and RAM usage) fluctuates due to background processes, standard operating systems may momentarily pause the data ingestion thread, leading to catastrophic sample dropping in a continuous bio-signal stream. To mitigate this in complex embedded nodes (such as the aggregation microprocessors in heterogeneous designs), the system kernel must be optimized for real-time performance. Techniques such as employing the \textit{PREEMPT\_RT} patch in Linux environments force the kernel to become fully preemptible, elevating the priority of hardware interrupt servicing above non-critical user-space tasks. Coupled with continuous resource profiling and core isolation (\textit{CPU shielding}), this ensures that the data aggregation pathway remains immune to thermal throttling or sudden processing spikes, thereby preserving the structural integrity and continuity of the neurophysiological record over extended monitoring periods. These real-time computing principles directly support the robust embedded acquisition architecture and the hardware-level temporal synchronization framework. Their practical implementation---including continuous CPU and RAM monitoring, PREEMPT\_RT kernel patching, and core isolation---is validated empirically through computational stress tests and end-to-end latency quantification.

In summary, this chapter has established the theoretical foundations required to address the two critical failures identified in the problem statement. The neurophysiological basis for ADHD assessment demands the faithful capture of microvolt-level ERPs and spontaneous cortical rhythms, which in turn requires optimized physical interfaces, robust mixed-signal hardware architectures, and deterministic temporal synchronization. The following chapters translate these principles into the concrete hardware design and the firmware-level implementation and validation of the MONEEE system.
